1. Defining the problem statement

Domain

Food Industry

Real world Problem

To further their education or find employment, many students and employees travel to various cities. Such a group of individuals will completely alter their way of life, including their accommodations, diets, and modes of transportation. Many of my classmates who moved to my institution from Andhra Pradesh are residing in PGs and can afford to eat out every day. We may thus regularly send meals as a remedy for this.

But how does foodoo differ from other food delivery applications currently available?

However, because they are caring for their families and working in the kitchen, many Indian housewives who want to start a business in order to support their families are unable to do so. Many mothers, housewives, cooks, and others who don't work may generate money from home while providing food for other people.

Our objective is to bring these two end users together in a way that benefits them both.

How?

This software need to be able to connect both end users.

User B may prepare their own food in the kitchen and launch a small company while User A can use this app to order handmade cuisine.

The application should also include an area where users may sell food as well as buy other food, track their orders, and have a wallet for internal financing.

Solution

"An app that allows users to purchase handmade foods directly from other users who prepare and sell them"

By doing so, user A can get healthier, save time and money, and user B may launch a small company from the comfort of their own home.

2. Understanding the user

User Persona

The user always comes first in the field of UX design. But, in order to prioritise user demands, we must first understand who we're talking about. So, let's have a look at personalities.

Personas are fictional users whose goals and characteristics represent the needs of a larger group of users. As a UX Designer You'll need to assess, investigate, and construct personas to represent major user groups as a UX designer. Similar replies, habits, or traits shared by several interview participants are used to create these user groups.

This implies that they reflect a wider group of users that share similar viewpoints, attitudes, and personality characteristics. Understanding that several consumers fall into the same broad group aids in defining the sorts of individuals for whom you're developing. That's where personas come in.

User Story

A fictional one-sentence story told from the persona's point of view to inspire and inform design decisions. User are short and impactful, with a clear action and benefit. The user stories you create will be very valuable as you start designing your product.

User stories can also serve as a “checklist” to make sure you're addressing and solving the key problems your users might face when engaging with your product.

User Journey Map

In its most basic version, journey mapping begins by creating a timeline from a sequence of user actions. After then, user emotions and feelings are added to the timeline in order to build a story. This story has been streamlined and compressed, and it ends with a visualisation.

A visual representation of a customer using your product or service is referred to as a user journey map. Although there is a case to be made that the term "customer" does the method a disservice , alignment on the term "map" is far less significant than alignment on the content contained within the map.

User Flow

A user flow is a visual representation of the various paths that can be taken when using an app or website, which can be written out or created digitally. The flowchart starts with the consumer's first interaction with the product, and finishes with the final action or result.

User flows also make it simple to describe the product's flow to your stakeholders and provide them a basic idea of how the interface you've designed is supposed to behave in its most efficient form.

By assisting your design team in visualizing how users will interact with the product, you can ensure that everyone is on the same page, resulting in a more productive and enjoyable work environment. User flows depict these potential patterns in a way that allows designers to evaluate the efficiency of the interface they're working on.

3. Starting the Design

Paper Wireframes

A wireframe is a low-fidelity outline of a digital experience, like an app or website.

Purposes of wireframes:

• Establish the basic structure of a page

• Highlight the intended function of the product

• Save time and resources

The wireframes will be made up of elements. Elements are building blocks used for creating a design. Most of the time, you'll use lines, shapes, and text to draw wireframes.

• Text is represented by horizontal lines.

• Images, photos, illustrations, and icons are represented by a square or rectangle and an X overlapping the square or rectangle.

• Calls-to-action are often represented by rectangles or circles. A common example of a call-to-action is a “submit” button on a web form.

Wireframes benefit you as the designer; they enable you to:

• Identify the content to include in your product

• Catch problems early

• Get stakeholders to focus on structure

• Save time and effort

• Iterate quickly

Low Fidelity Wireframes

A quick and simple method for turning high-level design concepts into observable and testable prototypes is low-fidelity (lo-fi) prototyping. Lo-fi prototypes' primary and most crucial job is to test and verify functioning, not the product's aesthetic appeal.

Here are the basic characteristics of low-fidelity prototyping:

Visual design: Only a few of the final product's visual characteristics are displayed (such as shapes of elements, basic visual hierarchy, etc.).

Content: Only the most important parts of the content are presented.

Interactivity: A real person can imitate the prototype. During a testing session, a specific person who is knowledgeable about design assumes the role of a computer and manually modifies the design's state in real-time. Wireframes, commonly referred to as "connected wireframes" can be used to generate interactivity.

5.Refining the Design

Affinity Diagram

An Affinity Diagram is a method for organizing enormous volumes of linguistic data (ideas, views, and concerns) into groups based on their natural links. The Affinity technique is frequently used to group ideas that have been developed through brainstorming.

The Affinity method is a fantastic technique to get individuals to collaborate on a creative level to solve problems. It may be employed in scenarios that are unfamiliar or unexplored by a team, as well as in situations that appear unclear or unorganized, such as when people with different backgrounds establish a new team or when members have insufficient expertise of the analytical area.

Themes based on Affinity diagram

While preliminary interview of the FooDoo app, participants from various fields age group participated, those participants where

1) It was observed that 3 out of 5 participants were struggling to find homemade food section. This means that many people gets lost.

2) It was observed that 4 out of 5 participants finds easy to place and track orders. This means that most of them finds order and tracking section is enjoyable.

3) It was observed that 2 out of 5 participants finds long changing address and placing order. This means that few think its long process.

4) It was observed that 3 out of 5 participants to add on certain features. This means that more than half thinks more features can be added on.

Insights based on themes

Based on the observed themes we extract the adequate insights onorder for the beterment of the user experience.

1) Based on the theme that: many people gets lost, an insight is: Users cues icons at the navigation bar should be named.

2) Based on the theme that: most of them finds order and tracking section is enjoyable, an insight is: Users feel orders and tracking section can be same.

3) Based on the theme that: few think its long process, an insight is: User cues Address bar should be more visible.

4) Based on the theme that: that more than half thinks more features can be added on, an insight is: User cues that more features can be added for great experience.

These Insights are further implemented while designing a Hi-Fi prototype

High Fidelity Wireframes

The degree of features and functionality incorporated into a prototype is referred to as the fidelity of the prototype. In this sense, a high-fidelity prototype is an interactive computer-based depiction of the product that comes the closest to the final design in terms of features and functionality. It is sometimes referred to as high-fidelity or hi-fidelity. High-fidelity refers to a level of comprehensiveness that enables in-depth analysis of usability issues and the drawing of inferences on user behaviour.

The high-fidelity prototypes encompass both the user experience (UX) and user interface (UI) components of the product, including interactions, user flow, and user behaviour.
